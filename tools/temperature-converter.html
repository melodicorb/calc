About Temperature Scales

Temperature scales are used to measure the degree of heat or cold in an object or environment. Different temperature scales are used around the world, with Celsius being the most widely adopted international standard.

Celsius (°C)

The Celsius scale, also known as the centigrade scale, is used in most countries around the world. On this scale:

  • Water freezes at 0°C
  • Water boils at 100°C (at standard atmospheric pressure)
  • Normal human body temperature is about 37°C
  • Room temperature is typically around 20-25°C

Fahrenheit (°F)

The Fahrenheit scale is primarily used in the United States. On this scale:

  • Water freezes at 32°F
  • Water boils at 212°F (at standard atmospheric pressure)
  • Normal human body temperature is about 98.6°F
  • Room temperature is typically around 68-77°F

Kelvin (K)

The Kelvin scale is used primarily in scientific contexts. It's an absolute temperature scale, meaning 0K is absolute zero - the theoretical absence of all thermal energy. On this scale:

  • Absolute zero is 0K (equivalent to -273.15°C or -459.67°F)
  • Water freezes at 273.15K
  • Water boils at 373.15K (at standard atmospheric pressure)
  • The Kelvin scale uses the same increment size as Celsius

Conversion Formulas

  • Celsius to Fahrenheit: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32
  • Fahrenheit to Celsius: °C = (°F - 32) × 5/9
  • Celsius to Kelvin: K = °C + 273.15
  • Kelvin to Celsius: °C = K - 273.15
  • Fahrenheit to Kelvin: K = (°F - 32) × 5/9 + 273.15
  • Kelvin to Fahrenheit: °F = (K - 273.15) × 9/5 + 32
